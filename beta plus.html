Let's explore Beta Plus Decay (β⁺) !!

In this page, we will explore Beta Plus Decay (β⁺ decay), another important process in nuclear physics.

Beta plus decay occurs when an unstable nucleus contains too many protons. To become more stable, a proton inside the nucleus converts into a neutron. During this process, a particle called a positron is emitted.

A positron is the antimatter version of an electron. It has the same mass as an electron but carries a positive charge instead of a negative charge.

During beta plus decay, the nucleus changes because a proton becomes a neutron. This decreases the atomic number of the element by one, helping the nucleus reach a more stable state.

Key Notes:

  • A positron is the antimatter equivalent of an electron.
  • Beta plus decay occurs when a proton converts into a neutron.
  • The atomic number decreases by 1.
  • This process helps stabilise proton-rich nuclei.
